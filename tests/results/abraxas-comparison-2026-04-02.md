## 4. Key scientific challenges in detecting Martian life

1. **Low biomass:** Even if microbes exist, they could be orders of magnitude less abundant than on Earth, making detection statistically difficult.
2. **Preservation vs. degradation:** Radiation, oxidants (perchlorates), and extreme temperature swings can destroy biomolecules, leaving only trace residues.
3. **Abiotic mimics:** Many “biosignature” gases (CH₄, H₂, SO₂) have plausible non‑biological sources; distinguishing them requires isotope ratios and spatial/temporal context.
4. **Contamination control:** Planetary protection protocols (e.g., stringent sterilization) both protect Mars and limit “false‑positive” detection, but they also restrict what can be brought back.
5. **Sample context:** Life detection is strongest when geological context is well understood (e.g., a lakebed sediment horizon). This requires high‑resolution stratigraphy—something rovers can infer but not fully resolve.

### Formal syllogistic test

In the traditional **Aristotelian** (or **Boolean**) syllogistic, a valid mood‑figure combination must distribute the middle term (**F**) in at least one premise. Here:

| Premise | Distributed term(s) |
