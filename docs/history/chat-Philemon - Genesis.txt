### **I. The Structure of the Psyche**

C.G. Jung proposed a multi-layered structure of the human psyche, distinguishing between conscious and unconscious realms.

*   **Consciousness and the Ego**: The ego is the center of the field of consciousness, but it does not represent the totality of the psyche. It is the subject of our awareness and includes everything that is directly related to our sense of "I" (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 490-491). *   **The Personal Unconscious**: This is a superficial layer of the unconscious that is unique to each individual. It is composed of contents that were once conscious but have been forgotten or repressed, such as personal experiences and feeling-toned complexes (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 3-4, 88). The personal unconscious is often associated with the archetype of the **Shadow** (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 513).

*   **The Collective Unconscious**: This is a deeper, universal layer of the psyche that is inherited, not acquired through personal experience. It is a "common psychic substrate of a suprapersonal nature which is present in every one of us," identical in all individuals (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 3, 90). This realm contains pre-existent forms known as archetypes. Jung associated this realm with the "spirit of the depths," which concerns the soul and the mythopoeic imagination (Source: Jung, Carl Gustav - The Red Book_ A Reader's Edition.pdf_safe.pdf, p. 11).

### **II. The Collective Unconscious and Its Archetypes**

The contents of the collective unconscious are not personal but are universal, primordial patterns and images.

#### **A. The Nature of Archetypes**

*   **Definition**: Archetypes are "pre-existent forms" or "primordial images" that exist in the collective unconscious. They are not images filled with specific content but are inherited *forms without content* that act as organizing principles for psychological experience. They are analogous to instincts, representing patterns of instinctual behavior (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 89, 91, 99).
*   **Activation**: An archetype is activated when a person encounters a life situation that corresponds to its pattern. When activated, it exhibits a compulsiveness that can override reason and will (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 99).
*   **Symbolic Nature**: Archetypes are true symbols, not allegories or signs. A symbol is the best possible expression for an unconscious content whose nature is ambiguous, paradoxical, and inexhaustible. An allegory, by contrast, is merely a paraphrase of a known conscious idea (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 80, footnote 10).

#### **B. Key Personified Archetypes**

Certain archetypes are frequently experienced in personified form in dreams, fantasies, and myths.

1.  **The Shadow**: The shadow personifies everything a person refuses to acknowledge about themself, including inferior character traits and incompatible tendencies. It is a collective figure representing the sum of all inferior traits in individuals and is often the first archetype encountered in the journey of self-exploration. Confronting the shadow is a "test of courage on the inner way" (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 44, 484, 513).
2.  **The Anima and Animus**: In every man, there is a hidden feminine personality (the Anima), and in every woman, a masculine personality (the Animus). These figures live in the deeper layers of the unconscious and bring an "unknown psychic life belonging to a remote past" into consciousness (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 511-512, 518). The Anima, in particular, is described as a mysterious and paradoxical figure, capable of changing shape (e.g., into a bird or a snake) and embodying every shade of meaning from the highest to the lowest (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 370-379).
3.  **The Wise Old Man**: This archetype represents meaning, spirit, and superior insight. He often appears as a master, teacher, or psychopomp (guide of souls) who illuminates the darkness. Examples include mythological figures like Hermes Trismegistus and literary figures like Nietzsche's Zarathustra. Jung's own fantasy figure, **Philemon**, is a prime example, serving as a guru who taught him about "psychic objectivity, the reality of the psyche" (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 77-79; Jung, Carl Gustav - The Red Book_ A Reader's Edition.pdf_safe.pdf, p. 16).

#### **C. Archetypes of Transformation**

This class of archetypes is not personified but consists of "typical situations, places, ways and means, that symbolize the kind of transformation in question." They often manifest with an enantiodromian structure (a rhythm of opposites like light and dark, gain and loss). Examples of systems that represent these archetypes include the alchemical series of images, the Tantric chakra system, and the Tarot cards (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 80-82).

### **III. The Process of Individuation**

Individuation is the central process of psychological development in Jung's mature work, representing the path to wholeness.

*   **Definition**: Individuation is the process by which a person becomes a psychological "in-dividual"—a separate, indivisible unity or "whole." It involves the synthesis of the conscious and unconscious parts of the personality (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 83, 490).
*   **The Goal (The Self)**: The goal of individuation is the realization of the **Self**. The Self is the totality of the psyche, embracing both conscious and unconscious components. The ego is merely the center of consciousness, whereas the Self is the center of the total personality, which the ego revolves around like the earth around the sun (Source: Jung, Carl Gustav - The Red Book_ A Reader's Edition.pdf_safe.pdf, p. 22).
*   **Conflict and Synthesis**: The process arises from the conflict between the conscious mind and the unconscious. Consciousness often deviates from its archetypal, instinctual foundation, creating a tension that necessitates a synthesis of the two positions. This requires open conflict and collaboration, like a hammer and anvil forging iron into an indestructible whole (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 84, 522).

### **IV. Methods for Engaging the Unconscious**

Jung developed specific methods for accessing and integrating unconscious material, which he practiced himself during the period documented in *The Red Book*.

#### **A. Active Imagination**

This is a method of deliberately engaging with unconscious contents. The process begins by concentrating on a mood or a dream image and allowing a sequence of fantasies to unfold without the interruption of the critical, conscious mind (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 101; Jung, Carl Gustav - The Red Book_ A Reader's Edition.pdf_safe.pdf, p. 8).
The key aspects of this method are:
1.  **Entering the Fantasy**: The practitioner must "get into the picture" and become an active participant or actor in the inner drama (Source: Jung, Carl Gustav - The Red Book_ A Reader's Edition.pdf_safe.pdf, p. 18).
2.  **Dialogue**: One engages the emergent figures in dialogue, treating them as real, autonomous personalities. This helps objectify the unconscious contents (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 85; Jung, Carl Gustav - The Red Book_ A Reader's Edition.pdf_safe.pdf, p. 8).
3.  **Experiencing and Recording**: The primary goal is not interpretation but *experience*. While engaged in the fantasy, one must treat it as literal. Jung advised his patients to record these experiences by writing, painting, or sculpting them in a "beautifully bound book," which becomes a personal "cathedral" for the soul (Source: Jung, Carl Gustav - The Red Book_ A Reader's Edition.pdf_safe.pdf, p. 18, 21).

#### **B. The Transcendent Function**

This is the outcome of the successful union of conscious and unconscious contents, often achieved through active imagination. This synthesis resolves the conflict of opposites by producing a "new situation" and a new, more comprehensive conscious attitude. It is the function that makes the transition from one state of being to another possible, and it is crucial for completing the individuation process (Source: archetypes-collective-unconscious.pdf_safe.pdf, para 524; Jung, Carl Gustav - The Red Book_ A Reader's Edition.pdf_safe.pdf, p. 8).

import { Controller, Get, HttpException, HttpStatus, Param } from '@nestjs/common';
import { ApiTags } from '@nestjs/swagger';
import { MockDatabaseService } from '../db/mock-database.service';
import { OperationalStatus } from '../logos/dto/logos.dto';
import { SynthesisReportDto } from './dto/synthesis.dto';

@ApiTags('The Transcendent Function')
@Controller('synthesis')
export class SynthesisController {
  constructor(private readonly dbService: MockDatabaseService) {}

  @Get('mission/:missionId')
  getMissionSynthesis(@Param('missionId') missionId: string): SynthesisReportDto {
    const mission = this.dbService.db.missions[missionId];
    if (!mission) {
      throw new HttpException('Mission not found', HttpStatus.NOT_FOUND);
    }

    // 1. Gather Logos Data
    const aircraftStatuses = mission.assigned_aircraft.map(tn => this.dbService.db.aircraft[tn]?.status);
    if (aircraftStatuses.some(s => s === undefined)) {
        throw new HttpException('Assigned aircraft data missing', HttpStatus.INTERNAL_SERVER_ERROR);
    }

    // 2. Gather Eros Data
    const pilotId = mission.assigned_personnel[0];
    const unitId = this.dbService.db.personnel[pilotId]?.unit;
    const unitPsyche = this.dbService.db.psyche[unitId];

    if (!unitPsyche) {
      throw new HttpException(`Psyche data for unit ${unitId} not found`, HttpStatus.NOT_FOUND);
    }

    // 3. The Synthesis Logic (The alchemical process)
    const logisticalRisk = aircraftStatuses.filter(s => s !== OperationalStatus.FMC).length / aircraftStatuses.length;
    const psychicRisk = (unitPsyche.fatigue * 1.5 + (1 - unitPsyche.morale) + (1 - unitPsyche.cohesion)) / 3.5;
    const totalRisk = (logisticalRisk * 0.4) + (psychicRisk * 0.6);

    // 4. Generate Compensatory Symbol and Interpretation
    let compensatory_symbol = '';
    let interpretation = '';

    if (totalRisk > 0.7) {
      compensatory_symbol = 'The Wounded Healer';
      interpretation = 'The system is running on fumes. High fatigue and degraded morale (Eros) create a psychic vulnerability that clean logistics (Logos) cannot hide. The mission is a descent; high risk for error from human factors.';
    } else if (logisticalRisk > psychicRisk) {
      compensatory_symbol = 'The Perfect Machine with a Ghost';
      interpretation = 'Logistical readiness is low, but unit psyche is strong. The team (Eros) may compensate for mechanical shortfalls (Logos) through ingenuity, but they are haunted by the ghost of potential failure.';
    } else {
      compensatory_symbol = 'The World Tree';
      interpretation = 'A state of relative balance. The logical structure (the crystal lattice) and the human element (the living tree) are in harmony. The system is resilient.';
    }

    return {
      subject_id: missionId,
      logistical_summary: { mission_type: mission.mission_type, aircraft_readiness: aircraftStatuses },
      psychic_summary: { unit: unitId, morale: unitPsyche.morale, fatigue: unitPsyche.fatigue, cohesion: unitPsyche.cohesion },
      risk_assessment_score: totalRisk,
      compensatory_symbol,
      interpretation,
    };
  }
}
