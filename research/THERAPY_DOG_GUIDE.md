## 2. Requirements Overview

### General Requirements (All Organizations)

**Handler Requirements:**
- Minimum age: 18 (some allow junior handlers with adult supervision)
- Background check (criminal)
- Completion of handler education/training
- Commitment to regular visits

**Dog Requirements:**
- **Age:** Minimum 1 year old (some require 18 months)
- **Health:** Current vaccinations, annual veterinary clearance
- **Temperament:** Calm, friendly, patient, non-aggressive
- **Training:** Basic obedience mastery (sit, stay, come, down, heel)
- **Socialization:** Comfortable with strangers, loud noises, medical equipment

**Testing Components:**
- **Handling Assessment:** Leash control, commands, positioning
- **Temperament Evaluation:** Reaction to strangers, sudden noises, crowding
- **Skills Test:** Sit/stay/down with distractions, greeting behavior
- **Observation Visits:** 3-4 supervised facility visits (ATD model)

---

### Specific Skill Requirements

**Must Demonstrate:**
- Walk politely on loose leash
- Sit and stay in place (minimum 3 minutes)
- Come when called (with distractions)
- Down stay with handler out of sight
- Accept petting from strangers
- Ignore food on floor (leave it)
- Calm reaction to sudden noises
- Comfortable with medical equipment (wheelchairs, walkers, IV poles)
- No jumping on people
- No mouthing or nipping

## 4. Border Collie-Specific Considerations

### Breed Strengths for Therapy Work

✅ **High Intelligence** - Quick learning, retains training well  
✅ **Trainability** - Eager to please, responsive to commands  
✅ **Athleticism** - Good stamina for visits  
✅ **Focus** - Can maintain attention on tasks  
✅ **Sensitivity** - Reads human emotions well  

---

### Breed Challenges & Management

⚠️ **High Energy** - Must be adequately exercised BEFORE visits  
- **Solution:** 30-60 minute vigorous exercise prior to therapy work
- Mental stimulation (puzzle toys, training games) essential

⚠️ **Herding Instinct** - May try to herd patients, children, or staff  
- **Solution:** Train "leave it" and "place" commands
- Redirect to appropriate behaviors
- Avoid environments with running children if instinct is strong

⚠️ **Sensitivity/Overstimulation** - Can become stressed in chaotic environments  
- **Solution:** Gradual exposure, watch for stress signals
- Know when to end visit
- Provide decompression time after visits

⚠️ **Work Drive** - May become frustrated if not "doing" enough  
- **Solution:** Give clear jobs during visits (hold stay, accept petting)
- Keep visits structured
- End on positive note

---

### Socialization Priorities for Border Collies

**Critical Exposure:**
- Wheelchairs, walkers, medical equipment
- Sudden movements (tremors, spasms)
- Loud noises (medical alarms, equipment)
- Crowded spaces
- Different flooring surfaces
- Elevators
- Various ages (pediatric to geriatric)
- People with disabilities

**Timeline:** Start socialization early (puppyhood ideal), but adult dogs can be resocialized with patience.

## 5. Step-by-Step Training Plan

### Month 1-2: Foundation Building

**Focus:** Basic Obedience Mastery

**Skills to Master:**
- Sit (3-second hold → 30-second hold)
- Down (1-minute hold → 5-minute hold)
- Stay (with distance: 5 feet → 20 feet)
- Come (with distractions)
- Heel (loose leash walking)
- Leave it (food, toys, distractions)
- Place/Go to mat (settle on command)

**Training Schedule:**
- 15-20 minutes, 2-3x daily
- High-value rewards (chicken, cheese, play)
- End each session on success

**Border Collie Note:** Use mental games to tire them. Training IS exercise for this breed.

---

### Month 3-4: CGC Preparation

**Focus:** Canine Good Citizen Test Skills

**AKC CGC Test Items:**
1. Accepting friendly stranger
2. Sitting politely for petting
3. Appearance and grooming (brushing, handling)
4. Out for a walk (loose leash)
5. Walking through a crowd
6. Sit/down on command with stay
7. Coming when called
8. Reaction to another dog
9. Reaction to distractions
10. Supervised separation (3 minutes)

**Action:** Find CGC evaluator in Davis County, schedule test.

---

### Month 5-6: Therapy-Specific Skills

**Focus:** Therapy Dog Behaviors

**New Skills:**
- **Settle on cue** - Lie down and relax on command
- **Paw/hand targeting** - For patients to pet
- **Chin rest** - Calm head placement on lap/wheelchair
- **Ignore medical equipment** - Wheelchairs, IV poles, walkers
- **Tolerance of awkward petting** - Rough handling, grabbing
- **Quiet on cue** - No barking in facility
- **Load into elevator** - Calm confined space behavior
- **Ignore food on floor/tables** - Critical in healthcare settings

**Practice Environments:**
- Visit busy stores (Home Depot, pet stores)
- Practice in parking lots with distractions
- Invite friends to simulate patient interactions

---

### Month 7-8: Mock Visits & Observation

**Focus:** Real-World Simulation

**Activities:**
- Arrange practice visits with friends/family
- Visit nursing home as visitor (no dog initially)
- Observe existing therapy dog teams
- Contact ATD Tester/Observer for evaluation readiness
- Complete handler education course

**Documentation:**
- Start visit log (even practice visits)
- Photograph training progress
- Collect vet records

---

### Month 9-10: Certification Process

**Focus:** Testing & Paperwork

**Steps:**
1. Contact ATD (or chosen org) for application
2. Complete background check
3. Schedule handling assessment with Tester/Observer
4. Complete 3-4 observation visits (supervised)
5. Submit paperwork and fees
6. Receive certification

---

### Month 11-12: First Independent Visits

**Focus:** Building Experience

**Goals:**
- Schedule first independent facility visits
- Aim for 2 visits/month minimum
- Debrief after each visit (what went well, what to improve)
- Maintain training (don't let skills slip)
- Join therapy dog community (Facebook groups, local meetups)

## 7. Legal Requirements & Insurance

### Utah State Laws

**Research Needed:** Utah-specific therapy animal laws should be verified. General principles:

- Therapy dogs are NOT service dogs (different legal protections)
- No public access rights without facility invitation
- Facility can set their own requirements
- Liability falls on handler/organization

**Key Distinction:**
- **Service Dog:** ADA protected, public access, task-trained for disability
- **Therapy Dog:** Facility invitation only, provides comfort to others
- **Emotional Support Animal:** Housing/travel protections only, no public access

---

### Liability Insurance

**Coverage Provided by Organizations:**
- ATD: Included with membership
- Pet Partners: Included with registration
- TDI: Included with membership

**Coverage Typically Includes:**
- Bodily injury (if dog bites/scratches)
- Property damage
- Legal defense costs
- **Limit:** Usually $100,000-$1,000,000 per occurrence

**Important:**
- Insurance only valid during authorized visits
- Must follow organization guidelines
- Report incidents immediately

---

### Facility Requirements

**Common Facility Requirements:**
- Proof of certification (current)
- Health clearance (annual)
- Vaccination records (current)
- Liability insurance (through organization)
- Background check (handler)
- Visit scheduling/approval process
- HIPAA compliance (in healthcare settings)
- Facility-specific orientation

## Final Notes for Border Collie Handlers

**Your Border Collie's Success Depends On:**

1. **Exercise First** - Never attempt therapy work with an under-exercised Border Collie
2. **Mental Jobs** - Give them clear tasks during visits (stay, watch, settle)
3. **Know Their Limits** - Watch for stress: panting, whale eye, lip licking, avoidance
4. **End Positive** - Always finish visits on a high note
5. **Decompress** - Allow quiet time after visits
6. **Stay Consistent** - Regular visits build confidence and routine

**Remember:** Not every Border Collie is suited for therapy work. If your dog shows consistent stress, anxiety, or overstimulation despite proper management, therapy work may not be the right fit. That's okay—there are other ways to use your dog's talents (dog sports, hiking, advanced obedience, etc.).
